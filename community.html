Frequently Asked Questions

At 8 months, most babies need 3–5 nursing sessions or 24–32 oz of formula per day, plus 2–3 solid food meals. Watch for hunger cues like rooting or sucking on hands. Feeding schedules can vary — follow your baby's lead while maintaining consistent timing.

Babies 6–12 months typically need 12–16 hours of sleep per 24-hour period (including naps). Most 8-month-olds take 2 naps (totaling 3–4 hours) and sleep about 10–12 hours at night. Establish a calming bedtime routine to improve sleep quality.

According to WHO standards, the average weight for an 8-month-old girl is approximately 7.5 kg (50th percentile), with a healthy range between 6.3–9.1 kg. Weight alone is not the only indicator of health — consult your pediatrician for a complete assessment using growth charts.

By 8 months, babies typically have received vaccines at birth, 2, 4, and 6 months. Common vaccines in this period include HepB, DTaP, IPV, Hib, PCV13, and Rotavirus. The flu vaccine (influenza) is recommended annually starting at 6 months. Always follow your pediatrician's schedule.

Most pediatricians recommend introducing solid foods around 6 months. Signs of readiness include sitting with support, showing interest in food, and loss of the tongue-thrust reflex. Start with single-ingredient purees, then gradually introduce textures and variety. Avoid honey, cow's milk as a drink, and choking hazards.

By 8 months, most babies can sit without support, bear weight on legs when held upright, say "mama" or "dada" (not always specific), pick up small objects with all fingers, and look for partially hidden objects. Every baby develops at their own pace — consult your doctor if you have concerns.

Sleep regression is a period when a baby who was sleeping well suddenly wakes more frequently. Common at 4, 8, and 12 months, it often coincides with developmental leaps. Maintain consistent routines, offer comfort without creating new sleep dependencies, and remember it is temporary (usually 2–6 weeks).
